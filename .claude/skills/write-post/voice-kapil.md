# Kapil's voice

Kapil's writing is compressed, high-context, and action-oriented. Operator/creative-lead blend: part PMM, part product/design lead, part founder. Closest references: modern startup founders on Slack/Twitter, design-forward PMMs, creative directors giving live feedback, product operators at Stripe / Linear / Vercel / Notion / Postman.

Strongest mode: **concise strategic framing with strong aesthetic instinct and clear business intent.**

## Defining traits

- **Direct and outcome-driven.** Writes to move something forward, not to discuss. "can we fix X?", "give me options", "make this more succinct".
- **Minimal ceremony.** Skips greetings, transitions, framing unless strategic. Feedback is concise: "too verbose", "closer", "this is weak", "more value-forward".
- **Iterative creative direction.** Refines through contrast: "less corporate", "more cinematic", "warmer", "too feature-heavy", "make it feel inevitable".
- **High-context shorthand.** Sentence fragments. Layered asks in one message. Quick pivots mid-thought. No strict grammar.
- **Taste-driven.** Calibrates by feel: "Stripe-quality", "enterprise-grade but approachable", "calm confidence", "cheerful and bright", "not overdesigned".
- **Succinct.** One-liners, Slack-ready blurbs, LinkedIn hooks, short bullets, punchy positioning.
- **Conversational but authoritative.** Doesn't hedge, doesn't sound corporate. Conviction without ceremony.
- **Structured thinking under casual syntax.** Funnel analysis, positioning layers, feature tiering, GTM sequencing, narrative hierarchy.

## How that lands in long-form (blog posts)

- **Lead with the why in one line.** No throat-clearing. The first paragraph states what's happening; the second states why this post exists.
- **Short paragraphs.** 1 to 3 sentences. Fragments earn their place.
- **Headers do work.** Each H2 is a stake in the ground: "The story changed", "Show, don't tell", "We're here for the long run". Not "Background", not "Introduction".
- **Bolded ledes inside sections.** When listing reasons or pillars, lead each with a bold one-liner, then a sentence or two of substance.
- **Concrete > abstract.** Real numbers ($3,762/yr, 98% of Fortune 500, 9 SDK languages). Real names. Real tools.
- **Outcomes, not architecture.** Describe what the reader gets, not how the system works.
- **Confident futures.** Use "will" sparingly and only for stated commitments. Avoid hedge words: "can", "may", "is designed to", "aims to".
- **Present indicative.** "The site does the work" not "the site will do the work".
- **Second person to the reader, first plural for Fern.** "We picked the harder path." "If you want to argue with any of this, DM me."
- **Close with an action.** A link to try it, a DM invitation, a hiring nudge. Never a summary paragraph.

## Don'ts

- No em-dashes. Use periods, commas, colons, parens, or just rephrase. (Colons are fine for short list-style intros: "Practically: more engineers, faster shipping.")
- No corporate filler. No "we are excited to announce", no "in today's fast-paced world", no "leverage", no "unlock value".
- No future hedging. Drop "can", "may", "is designed to".
- No vague adjectives. "Powerful", "robust", "seamless" mean nothing. Replace with a concrete behavior or proof point.
- No "10x" without a number. No "trusted by" without names.
- No AI-features in the abstract. Always named: Ask Fern, Fern Writer, MCP server, llms.txt.
- No trailing summary recap at the end. The reader read it.
